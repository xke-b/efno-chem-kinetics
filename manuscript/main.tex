\begin{abstract}
Neural surrogates for stiff chemistry are increasingly attractive for reacting-flow simulation, but their practical value depends on more than offline fit. This manuscript studies an EFNO-oriented implementation and deployment workflow for H$_2$ and C$_2$H$_4$ chemistry inside DeepFlame, with emphasis on coupled usefulness under explicit runtime contracts. Four conclusions are supported by the present evidence. First, implementation details matter enough to change scientific interpretation: on the H$_2$ benchmark, a transformed-state decode bug made self-rollout appear much less stable than it became after correction. Second, offline success does not transfer automatically to CFD deployment: DeepFlame-coupled H$_2$ runs fail first through thermodynamic reconstruction, which makes operating windows, fallback logic, and guard policies central parts of the method. Third, a credible partial deployment story is now available for H$_2$: the corrected branch, combined with frozen-temperature gating and an optional logistic risk guard, yields reproducible retention-oriented and safety-oriented operating modes. Fourth, the dominant remaining C$_2$H$_4$ limitation is not simply model capacity but target semantics: the best current mixed-data model survives to the stock runtime horizon, yet still over-drives cooler active cells, exhibits hot-regime source-term sign errors, and suppresses key intermediates, while naive chemistry-proxy relabeling fixes semantics too bluntly to preserve the solver-relevant manifold. The paper is therefore framed as a partial-results implementation study whose main contributions are diagnostic: contract-sensitive reproduction, deployment-facing validation, and explicit evidence that target semantics govern the remaining C$_2$H$_4$ gap.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Conventional stiff chemistry integration remains one of the main computational bottlenecks in high-fidelity reacting-flow simulation. Recent deep-learning surrogates offer a plausible alternative, but the question that matters for CFD practice is not only whether a network can fit one-step chemistry data offline; it is whether the resulting model can survive coupled deployment under the thermodynamic, postprocessing, and runtime constraints of a real solver \cite{li2025comprehensive,li2026dfodekit,wu2025nh3h2}. That distinction motivates the present study.

This paper examines an EFNO-oriented workflow for H$_2$ and C$_2$H$_4$ chemistry in DeepFlame, but its main contribution is not a blanket claim that one neural architecture has won. Instead, the work asks a more operational question: what has to be true for a learned stiff-chemistry surrogate to become solver-usable in CFD? The evidence developed here suggests three necessary ingredients. First, the implementation contract must be correct, especially when states are transformed before learning. Second, evaluation must be deployment-facing, because offline rollout scores can still disagree with solver behavior once postprocessing and thermodynamic reconstruction are applied. Third, the target labels must reflect the chemistry one actually wants the network to learn; otherwise even runtime-stable models can converge to the wrong reactive manifold.

Those themes appear differently in the two fuels studied here. For H$_2$, the strongest current result is a deployment-oriented one: once a key transformed-state decode bug is fixed, the corrected branch becomes a credible basis for guarded DeepFlame deployment, and the decisive design choices become fallback logic, activation threshold, and runtime contract rather than one more offline sweep. For C$_2$H$_4$, by contrast, the strongest current result is still diagnostic: after the runtime bridge is stabilized, the remaining error is tightly structured around target semantics, intermediate suppression, and source-term sign errors rather than mere survivability. The paper is therefore written as a rigorous partial-results study whose value lies in identifying reproducible operating regimes, failure modes, and design rules rather than in claiming solved surrogate chemistry replacement across all coupled settings.

\section{Background and related work}
The manuscript is grounded in the EFNO paper by Weng et al. \cite{weng2025efno} and a local-paper crosswalk covering data generation, augmentation, and coupled-validation issues from related combustion-chemistry learning papers \cite{li2025comprehensive,xiao2026physicsaware,li2026dfodekit,wu2025nh3h2}. Across these papers, a recurring theme is that model architecture alone is not decisive: data semantics, postprocessing contracts, augmentation strategy, and a posteriori validation all materially affect conclusions.

\section{Reproducible workflow and software stack}
The implementation workflow combines PDF extraction and structured notes, DFODE-kit extensions for EFNO-style training and evaluation, and DeepFlame export bridges for MLP and FNO-like surrogates. The software path is deliberately aligned with the emerging DFODE-kit/DeepFlame ecosystem \cite{li2026dfodekit,li2025comprehensive}: data are prepared from canonical or case-aligned thermochemical states, models are trained and evaluated offline with explicit rollout and physical-consistency metrics, and promising checkpoints are exported into DeepFlame for coupled testing. All substantive experiments are documented in the project docs site together with scripts and artifact paths.

\section{Methods and evaluation protocol}
The study is organized as a staged implementation-and-deployment workflow rather than a single train/test exercise. The offline H$_2$ benchmark uses autoignition trajectories generated from a seven-species hydrogen mechanism, with holdout splits defined by initial-condition trajectories rather than random sample shuffling so that rollout claims reflect unseen trajectory behavior rather than interpolation within the same paths. Models are compared with fixed seeds and evaluated on one-step thermochemical error, long-horizon rollout error, and physical-consistency metrics including element-mass and mass-sum deviations. This choice follows directly from the EFNO paper's emphasis on autoregressive prediction under unseen conditions \cite{weng2025efno}, but the present implementation also makes the evaluation contract explicit because several apparently strong branches change rank once deployment-facing postprocessing is enforced.

For DeepFlame-coupled H$_2$, the target case is the Burke nine-species configuration rather than the earlier seven-species offline mechanism. That mechanism/species-count alignment became a hard requirement once export began. Coupled evaluations are therefore performed on Burke-aligned exported checkpoints and interpreted under the same species postprocessing contract used by the runtime bridge. In practice, this means distinguishing the project-side closure reconstruction from the DeepFlame-facing ``preserve-last'' rule, where the last species is preserved from the current state and the predicted main species are renormalized. This contract choice materially compresses some offline ranking gaps and is therefore part of the method, not an implementation footnote.

For C$_2$H$_4$, the baseline is a stock-style DeepFlame run in the trustworthy GPU regime (`mpirun -np 8`, `coresPerNode 8`) carried to $5\times10^{-6}$. Learned-model experiments are only interpreted after this runtime baseline and after the exported FNO bridge was repaired to batch active-set inference, because earlier reduced-rank GPU crashes and late out-of-memory failures showed that runtime pathologies could otherwise be mistaken for model failures. The main C$_2$H$_4$ learned datasets then span three increasingly deployment-relevant families: crude case-pair labels extracted from CFD states, filtered/mixed case-pair subsets such as $dp100$, and canonical/augmented data motivated by nearby literature on reactive-region coverage \cite{li2025comprehensive,xiao2026physicsaware,li2026dfodekit}. During this stage we also confirmed that DFODE-kit’s documented DeepFlame-backed one-dimensional freely propagating flame workflow can be instantiated for the C$_2$H$_4$ Wu24sp mechanism and sampled into HDF5, which creates a more faithful future starting point for Xiao-style one-dimensional flame sampling than the earlier Cantera-only canonical prototype. A direct manifold comparison then showed that the raw DeepFlame one-dimensional sample is a better physical backbone than the Cantera-only canonical sample in pressure support, but it still under-covers the reactive intermediate manifold that appears in the stock active set; conversely, the current canonical augmentation over-corrects in the opposite direction. Applying Xiao-style interpolation and constrained perturbation directly on top of the DeepFlame-sampled one-dimensional manifold moved the current-state support into a more balanced regime and produced a first deployed model that survived to $10^{-6}$, but the learned chemistry still deleted key intermediates such as C$_2$H$_5$, C$_2$H$_3$, CH$_2$CHO, and CH$_2$CO. A follow-on mixed-label test combining $dp100$ with a small one-dimensional augmented add-on likewise preserved early runtime survival without rescuing this chemistry gap. Importantly, a matched DeepFlame C$_2$H$_4$ reference run with standard chemistry integration (`torch off`) showed that the stock learned example remains much closer to the in-loop chemistry baseline at $10^{-6}$ than these current project surrogates: the stock model preserves the key intermediate manifold within order-one factors, whereas the current one-dimensional and mixed-label project models suppress those intermediates by many orders of magnitude while over-driving $\dot{Q}$. Replacing the current Box--Cox state-delta species target with a Xiao-style power transform on physical $\Delta Y$ materially improved bulk deployment behavior for the mixed $dp100$+one-dimensional case: the mean active-region $\dot{Q}$ ratio versus the CVODE baseline collapsed from about $8.6\times$ to $8.5\times10^{-4}$, while the mean temperature and pressure errors also dropped. However, this first power-delta deployment still left the key intermediate manifold effectively extinguished. This strengthens the case that one-dimensional DeepFlame augmentation is useful as a solver-native manifold source and that target transforms matter substantially, but not yet as a drop-in labeled-data fix without further regime-aware semantic refinement.

Across both fuels, the manuscript prioritizes deployment-facing metrics over raw fit alone. Offline results are summarized by one-step MAE and rollout MAE, but coupled H$_2$ decisions are further driven by HP-reconstruction failure fractions, learned-vs-fallback active-cell fractions, and threshold/guard operating windows. Coupled C$_2$H$_4$ decisions are driven by survival to the stock baseline horizon together with matched-mesh comparisons of $\dot{Q}$, temperature, pressure, and key species. This method deliberately treats negative results---for example, preserve-last rebaselining, pure chemistry-proxy early failure, and random partial relabeling regression---as evidence about evaluation contracts and target semantics rather than as dead ends.

\section{Offline H$_2$ reproduction and implementation diagnosis}
The offline H$_2$ study began as a reproduction exercise, but the main result became methodological rather than purely comparative. Seeded baseline runs initially suggested that rollout-aware EFNO-style training could outperform the supervised MLP reference by a wide margin on long-horizon metrics. However, branch-by-branch diagnosis of the second-step feature path showed that the apparent instability of self-fed rollout was dominated by a transformed-state species decode bug: species updates were being combined across incompatible state spaces. After correcting the update rule to act in the proper transformed space, the empirical picture changed qualitatively. Self-rollout branches that had previously looked intrinsically unstable became the strongest rollout performers, while teacher-forced rollout remained competitive rather than dominant.

Figure~\ref{fig:h2-corrected-decode} makes the scale of this change explicit. The correction was not a small numerical cleanup; it is the difference between concluding that self-feeding is fundamentally harmful on this benchmark and concluding that a large part of the previous failure was implementation-induced. This is an important paper-level point because it reframes ``reproduction'' as contract-sensitive implementation diagnosis: the benchmark is useful not only for ranking models, but for exposing which conclusions are robust to the details of transformed-state decoding and autoregressive feature construction.

\section{DeepFlame-coupled H$_2$: from failure to guarded deployment}
The coupled H$_2$ study then tested whether the improved offline story survived contact with a real CFD runtime. It did not, at least not directly. Exported Burke-aligned checkpoints loaded correctly and began evolving the case, but both supervised and rollout-aware branches encountered thermodynamic failure through HP reconstruction after only a few microseconds. That failure mode mattered because it shifted the central question away from offline accuracy and toward solver compatibility: the immediate problem was no longer whether the network predicted species well in isolation, but whether the coupled update remained thermodynamically reconstructable.

This failure, however, became informative rather than terminal. Snapshot analysis, written-field inspection, and explicit HP-risk reconstruction turned the crash into a measurable operating-window problem. Hybrid fallback policies, frozen-temperature gating, and a logistic risk guard were then introduced as deployment mechanisms rather than ad hoc hacks. Figure~\ref{fig:h2-operating-windows} shows how threshold and guard choices reshape the coupled operating regime over time. Table~\ref{tab:h2-deployment-summary} summarizes representative operating points for the corrected branch. In the present evidence base, $650$~K gives the strongest long-horizon single-threshold retention, while the logistic risk guard at threshold $0.5$ provides a more safety-oriented mode with lower HP-failure exposure at some cost in learned participation.

The H$_2$ evidence therefore supports a reviewable claim stronger than ``the corrected branch works better.'' It supports the narrower but more consequential claim that deployment-facing chemistry surrogates require contract alignment and explicit thermodynamic safeguards, not just better offline loss values. In other words, coupled usability emerges from the interaction between model, postprocessing rule, activation threshold, and fallback logic; no single offline metric captures that interaction by itself.

\section{C$_2$H$_4$ baseline establishment and runtime bridge diagnosis}
For C$_2$H$_4$, the first task was not model improvement but baseline legitimacy. Early reduced-rank CPU/GPU experiments were useful for diagnosing packaging and communication failures, yet they were not trustworthy scientific baselines because the stock example was clearly written around a different runtime regime. The program therefore re-established the case in the stock-style `np=8` GPU configuration and only then began treating the resulting trajectory as the deployment target. This distinction matters for the paper narrative: before a trustworthy stock baseline exists, it is too easy to misattribute infrastructure failure to chemistry-model failure.

The same logic applied to the learned runtime bridge. The first case-aligned FNO models could survive farther than the earliest homogeneous-data smoke baseline, but late out-of-memory failures and zero-$\dot{Q}$ written fields showed that the deployment path itself was still contaminating interpretation. Batching active-set inference repaired that bridge. Only after those runtime issues were removed could the remaining C$_2$H$_4$ discrepancies be interpreted as evidence about target quality rather than infrastructure. This emphasis on a posteriori validation follows the practice advocated in recent combustion-surrogate studies, which consistently show that apparently strong offline models can still fail once coupled back into CFD \cite{li2025comprehensive,xiao2026physicsaware,wu2025nh3h2}.

\section{C$_2$H$_4$ data semantics: what the current best model still gets wrong}
Once the C$_2$H$_4$ runtime path was stabilized, the central question shifted from ``can a learned model survive?'' to ``what chemistry is the current label path actually teaching?'' The strongest current data result is a calibrated mixed dataset that combines the solver-aligned $dp100$ case-pair backbone with a moderate canonical augmentation component. This mixed model survives to $5\times10^{-6}$ and is substantially better than earlier baselines, which is already an important positive result for the paper. But it does not fail in a random or weakly structured way. The best-model-vs-stock anatomy shows a coherent residual pathology: cooler active cells are over-driven, the hot bulk exhibits widespread source-term sign errors, and several late intermediates are still heavily suppressed. The mixed-data direction itself is motivated by nearby literature showing the value of canonical flame manifolds, interpolation-based densification, and physics-aware perturbation for building thermochemical training sets that cover reactive regions more effectively \cite{li2025comprehensive,xiao2026physicsaware,li2026dfodekit}.

However, deployment tests also showed that semantics-only fixes are not enough in isolation. A tiny pure chemistry-proxy model fails catastrophically by $4\times10^{-7}$, while naive partial replacement of 10\% of labels preserves runtime survival but badly degrades solver-facing quality.

Table~\ref{tab:c2h4-runtime-summary} makes the current C$_2$H$_4$ claim boundary explicit. The best mixed model is reviewably better than prior baselines, but naive partial chemistry-proxy relabeling is not yet a deployment improvement. Tables~\ref{tab:c2h4-error-anatomy} and \ref{tab:c2h4-chemproxy-mismatch} sharpen the interpretation in an important way. Together they suggest that the remaining gap is not primarily one of network capacity or another nearby mixing ratio; it is a semantic gap between the full-CFD labels available cheaply and the chemistry-only evolution that would better preserve the intermediate manifold. The negative controls are therefore informative: pure chemistry-proxy relabeling fixes semantics too aggressively and loses the solver-relevant manifold, while random partial relabeling preserves the manifold but injects the new semantics in the wrong places.

A new targeted relabeling scout now makes that selective-semantics direction more concrete. On a late-window $dp100$ subset, a regime-targeted selector that concentrates relabel budget on cooler/intermediate-rich rows raises the selected intermediate-species mass from $5.36\times10^{-4}$ for a random 3k subset to $3.13\times10^{-3}$, while shifting the mean temperature from $2213$~K to $1399$~K. One-step chemistry-proxy relabeling on this targeted subset then shows an even larger semantic gap than the earlier random early-window scout, with mean absolute $T_{next}$ difference about $656$~K and order-of-magnitude shifts on species such as HCCO and C$_2$H$_5$. Deployment tests then show that row choice and replacement strength interact strongly. A targeted 10\% partial-relabel model collapses by $4\times10^{-7}$ with severe thermodynamic excursions, while lowering the same targeted strategy to 2\% delays failure to $9\times10^{-7}$ but still does not recover solver-usable behavior. This refines the forward claim further. Selection quality matters, but simply injecting stronger chemistry-proxy semantics through direct substitution is not enough; replacement strength and staging likely matter as much as row choice. This supports the more specific forward hypothesis that semantics must be improved selectively rather than globally.

\section{Discussion}
The present evidence supports several broader lessons that cut across both fuels. First, transformed-state implementation details can dominate empirical conclusions, which reinforces the need to treat reproduction as implementation diagnosis rather than a narrow hyperparameter chase \cite{weng2025efno}. The H$_2$ decode-contract result is the clearest example: a bug in how states were updated across transformed spaces could easily have been misread as a limitation of rollout-aware learning itself.

Second, offline and deployment-facing evaluations can disagree substantially because of postprocessing and thermodynamic contract issues, in line with the broader combustion-surrogate literature's emphasis on a posteriori validation \cite{li2025comprehensive,wu2025nh3h2}. In this study, the preserve-last versus closure distinction changes offline H$_2$ rankings, and HP-reconstruction risk governs whether an apparently good checkpoint is solver-usable at all.

Third, the two fuels expose different limiting mechanisms. For H$_2$, the strongest current challenge is deployment control: the offline branch can be made good enough that guard design, threshold choice, and runtime contract become decisive. For C$_2$H$_4$, by contrast, the strongest current challenge is still target construction. Once runtime pathologies were removed, the residual errors remained tightly structured around source-term sign, intermediate suppression, and chemistry-versus-CFD label mismatch. That pattern argues against spending the next effort on another small architecture or ratio sweep.

Taken together, these lessons define the manuscript's central claim boundary. The paper does not show that learned chemistry has already replaced conventional integration in all relevant CFD settings. It does show that careful implementation, deployment-aware validation, and explicit treatment of target semantics can move the question from generic promise to concrete operating regimes, failure modes, and reproducible design rules.

\section{Conclusion}
The current project state is strong enough for a reviewable partial-results paper because it supports a concrete, bounded claim. For H$_2$, corrected implementation plus deployment safeguards yields a credible coupled story: once the transformed-state update contract is fixed, learned chemistry can be kept solver-usable in DeepFlame through explicit operating-window design rather than through offline accuracy alone. For C$_2$H$_4$, the strongest result is equally concrete but more limited: a calibrated mixed-data model now reaches the stock runtime horizon, yet its residual errors remain organized around target semantics, especially intermediate suppression and source-term sign mismatch, which shows that further progress depends more on selective relabeling and target design than on another small architecture sweep.

The broader conclusion is methodological. Learned stiff-chemistry surrogates should not be judged only as regressors on isolated datasets. They should be treated as coupled numerical components whose usefulness depends on implementation correctness, postprocessing contracts, thermodynamic reconstructability, and the meaning of the target labels themselves. Under that standard, the present study does not claim that neural surrogates have already replaced conventional chemistry integration across all relevant CFD settings. It does claim that a deployment-aware workflow can turn that broad ambition into reproducible operating regimes, interpretable failures, and actionable design rules for the next generation of H$_2$ and C$_2$H$_4$ surrogate-chemistry models.
